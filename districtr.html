How hard is redistricting?

A basic note on hardness of redistricting

This project uses districtr to show the hardness of non-partisan redistricting.

Redistricting is all about drawing lines in a state to divide it into: (i) contiguous, and (ii) (roughly) same populated areas. This process occurs every 10 years and after the census determines the number of representatives in each state based on its population. Although there exists some other constitutional measures (rather than contiguity and population balance) to assess a redistricting plan, they look like general rules that are not specified precisely (in some states, the legislature use the term “to the extent practicable” for some measures (even for population balance)).

Redistricting is a hard problem even if you consider population bound as the only constraint on the redistricting map. It means that as the size of your redistricting instance increases, it becomes harder to be solved (even for super computers).

Now, we discuss on a well-known bright-line rule for non-partisan redistricting. It is population balance!

Population balance

Different states consider various rules on their population bounds of their districts. First, we introduce different population deviations.

  1. Singular deviation: This is the deviation from the ideal population for every district.
  2. Total deviation: This is the summation of deviations from ideal population for all districts.

For more information on population bound, click here. Here are some population bounds for some states based on A Citizen's Guide To Redistricting.
